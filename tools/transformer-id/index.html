Transformer ID Power · Output
⚠ DANGER — never apply mains voltage to an unknown transformer.
Use a known low-voltage AC source — a 6.3 V filament transformer, function generator, or a variac set very low. Apply it to a single winding, then probe the others with your meter on AC volts. If the tested winding gets warm, draws excessive current, hums loudly, or your readings are wildly off, disconnect immediately — you may have a shorted turn or a non-line-frequency winding.
Test setup

Units: all AC voltages here are VRMS — which is what your multimeter displays on AC volts for any sine-wave source (filament transformer, function generator on sine, mains). No special instrument or conversion needed.

  1. DC pre-test. Disconnect the transformer from everything and set your multimeter to ohms. For each winding, touch one probe to one end and the other probe to the other end — polarity doesn't matter for resistance. Three leads where the middle reads about half the outer-to-outer value = a center-tapped winding. Enter each reading in the rows below.
  2. AC test. Pick any one winding and connect your AC source (a 6.3 V filament transformer works well) across it — one source lead to each end. Set your meter to AC volts and probe every other winding the same way (one probe to each end of the winding you're reading). Polarity doesn't matter for AC.
  3. Calibrate. Enter the primary's measured AC reading here, along with the line voltage it was designed for. The tool scales every secondary reading up to its rated full-mains value.

Test setup

An output transformer has a primary (the high-impedance side that connects to the tube plates and B+ supply in a finished amp) and a secondary (the low-impedance side that connects to the speaker). The primary may have a center tap; the secondary may have several taps for different speaker loads.

Units: all AC voltages here are VRMS — what your multimeter displays on AC volts. The OT math only uses voltage ratios, so any consistent unit (peak, peak-to-peak, RMS) gives the same impedance answer — but RMS matches what a meter shows directly.

  1. DC pre-test. Set your multimeter to ohms. For each winding, touch one probe to each end — polarity doesn't matter. A primary reads 50–700 Ω (many turns of fine wire); a secondary speaker tap reads < 5 Ω (few turns of heavy wire). For a center-tapped primary, measure all three lead-pair combinations to find the center.
  2. AC test. Connect your AC source (~1 V from a function generator at 50–500 Hz) across one winding — one source lead to each end of that winding. Set your meter to AC volts and probe every other winding the same way (one probe to each lead). Polarity doesn't matter.
  3. Configure below. Pick a reference mode: Known anchor if you know one tap's impedance (e.g. it's the 8 Ω speaker output), or Auto-detect if the transformer is completely unknown — the tool then matches the voltage ratios against standard tap sets ({4, 8, 16} Ω, etc.) and infers each winding's impedance.
