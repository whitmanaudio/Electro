Glossary  ·  result cards & network
Stage terms  ·  V1A / V1B
gm transconductance · mA/V

Calculated: central finite difference on the Koren tube model at the DC operating point — gm = [Ip(Vp, Vgk+10 mV) − Ip(Vp, Vgk−10 mV)] / 20 mV.

Circuit impact: how much plate current moves per volt of grid swing — the engine of gain (μ = gm·rp). An operating-point property, not a tube constant: colder bias means lower gm.

Sonic levers: you steer gm through bias and tube choice. Shrink Rk (1.5k → 820 Ω) or raise B+ for a hotter bias — more gain, earlier breakup. Swap the 12AX7 for a 5751 or 12AY7 to drop gm: cleaner, more touch-dynamic front end (the classic vintage-amp clean-up move).

rp dynamic plate resistance · kΩ

Calculated: central difference on the same model — rp = 20 mV / [Ip(Vp+10 mV) − Ip(Vp−10 mV)] at fixed Vgk.

Circuit impact: the tube's internal AC resistance at the plate. Sets gain via Av = −μ·Ra/(rp+Ra), Ra = Rp ∥ Rload, and dominates Output Z.

Sonic levers: low-rp tubes (12AY7, 12AT7) drive the next network stiffly — tighter bass, less loading interaction. Raising Rp (100k → 220k) buys gain but adds "give"; watch the load line flatten on the plate-curves chart as you try it.

Input Z mid-band input impedance · kΩ / MΩ

Calculated: Zin = Rg (grid stopper) + grid return. V1A: assumed 1 MΩ source-side leak; V1B: the interstage pot's wiper-to-ground section, so it moves with Vol.

Circuit impact: the resistive load this stage presents to its driver at mid frequencies. The HF input impedance falls with Miller capacitance (Cga magnified by stage gain) — visible in the Bode charts, not in this number.

Sonic levers: the grid stopper is the practical knob. Raise Rg (68k → 220–470k) and the Miller corner drops — smoother, darker, less fizz under gain (a primary high-gain voicing trick). Lower it for brighter, sharper attack.

Output Z mid-band output impedance · kΩ

Calculated: bypassed cathode: rp ∥ Rp. Unbypassed: (rp + (μ+1)·Rk) ∥ Rp — cathode feedback raises the tube's effective plate resistance.

Circuit impact: the source impedance driving the next network — sets the coupling-cap high-pass corner, feeds the output node's looking-back Z, and scales how hard downstream grid current sags this stage.

Sonic levers: pull the bypass cap and Output Z roughly doubles — leaner, tighter, more "stringy" with the same parts. Smaller Rp or a lower-rp tube stiffens the drive, pushing fuller lows through the same coupling cap (the HP corner falls).

Output delivered swing · Vpp

Calculated: measured peak-to-peak of the settled time-domain simulation at this stage's coupling-cap output, at the current Input / Tone / Vol settings — clipping, compression and bias shift included.

Circuit impact: the swing actually delivered right now, as a scope would show it. The gap below Gain × input is your compression/clipping.

Use it: compare against the next stage's Max in: raise Vol until Output exceeds it and that's exactly where second-stage breakup begins. Designing a clean machine? Keep Output under downstream Max in with a few dB to spare.

Grid shift operating-point displacement · V

Calculated: mean(Vgk) over the settled capture window minus its quiescent value (−Vk).

Circuit impact: total DC displacement of the operating point under drive — blocking charge plus benign cathode sag and AC re-centering. Negative = pushed toward cutoff. The Block meter isolates just the grid-current part.

Sonic levers: a large negative value under drive is the "ducks after every hit" feel. Tame it with a smaller interstage cap, a bigger grid stopper on the driven stage, less drive into the stage, or a lower-μ tube with more bias headroom.

Bias excursion cathode drift · V

Calculated: mean(instantaneous Vk) over the settled window minus quiescent Vk — the bypass cap holds the cathode with τ = Rk·Ck, so asymmetric plate current charges it away from idle.

Circuit impact: slow self-rebias under sustained signal — the compression component of "tube feel." Positive = average plate current rose, stage drifted colder.

Sonic levers: bigger Ck (25µ → 100µ) lengthens the bloom/squash on held notes. Remove Ck and excursion vanishes — stiffer, faster feel and ~6 dB less gain. A partial bypass (0.68–1 µF) trades sag for a treble-tilted gain shelf (see the Ck curve in the Bode chart).

Block rec blocking recovery · ms

Calculated: the stage's input coupling cap × its discharge path. V1A: Cin × 1 MΩ. V1B: V1A's Cout × (Rseries + Rpot + Zout V1A).

Circuit impact: how long the stage stays choked after an overdriven transient. Caps don't block deeper when bigger — they choke longer. "—" on V1A means no input cap: a DC-coupled stage cannot block.

Sonic levers: THE tightness control for high gain. Drop the interstage cap 22n → 2.2n and recovery gets ~10× faster — palm mutes stay tight, no bass flub — at the cost of low end through that stage (the HP corner rises; check the Bode chart). 22n+ = full, vintage, slower to recover.

Network terms  ·  interstage & output
Attenuation interstage divider · dB

Calculated: 20·log10[Rpot_bot / (Rseries+Rpot)] at the current Vol position (resistive, mid-band; the bright cap modifies it at HF on the Bode chart).

Circuit impact: how much of V1A's output reaches V1B's grid.

Sonic levers: this is what the Vol slider does — it decides where the chain distorts. Low Vol: V1A's character only, V1B clean. High Vol: V1B gets slammed (watch its Block meter and clip readouts light up). A permanent Series R builds in attenuation for a cleaner-by-design second stage.

Source Z wiper Thevenin impedance · kΩ

Calculated: (Rseries+Rpot_top) ∥ Rpot_bot — zero at Vol 0, Rseries at full, peaking near mid-rotation (≈250 kΩ on a 1 M pot).

Circuit impact: the impedance feeding V1B's grid. With V1B's Miller capacitance it forms the dominant HF rolloff, and it sits in the blocking charge path (higher Z = gentler grid conduction).

Sonic levers: the classic "amp goes dull at half volume" lives here — the mid-rotation impedance peak against Miller capacitance. Cure it with a bright cap across the pot's top half (watch the V1B Bode chart lift), or use a smaller pot value for less peak at the cost of V1A gain.

V1A Rload interstage total resistance · Ω

Calculated: Rseries + Rpot — the wiper position doesn't change it (the wiper is unloaded at audio).

Circuit impact: V1A's AC load: sets its gain via Ra = Rp ∥ Rload and its clip ceilings, and scales the blocking recovery τ.

Sonic levers: swap the 1 M pot for 250 k and V1A loses a touch of gain and swing — subtly cleaner and faster-recovering from blocking; keep 1 M for maximum gain. Watch V1A's Gain and Max in readouts move as you change the pot value.

V1B grid leak DC grid return · kΩ

Calculated: Rpot_bot = Rpot × Vol fraction — the wiper-to-ground section of the interstage pot.

Circuit impact: V1B's DC grid reference and part of the coupling high-pass / blocking discharge path. At Vol 0 the grid is grounded and the stage is silent.

Sonic levers: mostly indirect — it rides with Vol, so the HP corner and blocking recovery drift slightly across the pot's travel, exactly like a real one-pot interstage. A design with a fixed 1 M grid leak after the pot would pin those corners regardless of volume.

Master atten output divider · dB

Calculated: same divider math as Attenuation, applied to the output network at the current Master position.

Circuit impact: output level after all the distortion has been generated — purely resistive here, so it scales amplitude without changing tone.

Sonic levers: the master-volume principle: set Input and Vol for the distortion voicing you want, then use Master purely for loudness. (In a full amp a low master also means less power-amp contribution — that part isn't modeled here.)

V1B Rload output-network total resistance · Ω

Calculated: output-network Rseries + Rpot — V1B's AC load, independent of the Master position.

Circuit impact: the same gain/ceiling lever for V1B that V1A Rload is for V1A.

Sonic levers: a smaller master pot (1 M → 250 k) trims V1B's gain and headroom — verify on the Gain readout. Use the output Series R to mimic the input impedance of whatever real stage follows, and the tool's clip readouts will reflect that loading.

Output Z looking back at the output node · kΩ

Calculated: (Rseries_out + Rpot_top + Zout V1B) ∥ Rpot_bot at the master wiper — zero at Master 0, peaking near mid-rotation.

Circuit impact: the source impedance presented to anything connected downstream — distinct from the per-stage Output Z cards (this one includes the whole output network).

Sonic levers: the interface number. Driving long cables or a pedal input from >100 kΩ here shaves treble (cable capacitance against this Z). Fixes: run Master high, use a smaller master pot, or plan a cathode-follower/buffer after this node.

Blocking charge τ choke onset speed · ms

Calculated: Cout V1A × [(Rseries+Rpot_top) + (Rg2+1 k) ∥ Rpot_bot] at the current wiper.

Circuit impact: how quickly blocking charge builds while V1B's grid conducts — the attack of the choke.

Sonic levers: a bigger V1B grid stopper or interstage Series R slows the charge — gentler choke onset when the stage is slammed. A smaller coupling cap shortens it, but also shortens recovery, which is usually the better trade (see Blocking recovery τ).

Blocking recovery τ choke release time · ms

Calculated: Cout V1A × (Rseries + Rpot + Zout V1A) — the same number as V1B's Block rec card.

Circuit impact: drain time of the accumulated blocking charge — how long the post-transient duck lasts.

Sonic levers: interstage cap size first, network resistance second. Roughly: ≤5 ms reads as tight modern high-gain; 20–50 ms is vintage-style looseness that "breathes" after big chords. Set the cap by where you want the low-end corner, then check this number tells the same story.
