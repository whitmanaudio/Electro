Glossary  ·  JFET stage & network
Device & bias
IDSS & VP device constants · mA, V

Source: the two numbers that define the Shockley square law ID = IDSS·(1−VGS/VP)². Device pick seeds typical values; overwrite both with your DCA75 reading of the actual part.

Circuit impact: IDSS is the drain current at VGS=0; VP (pinch-off / VGSoff, negative) is where the channel cuts off. Together they set bias, gm and the clean input window (±|VP| ballpark).

Why measure: JFET spreads are huge (J201 IDSS 0.2–1 mA, VP −0.3–−1.5 V). Two "J201s" can bias and clip very differently — matching/measuring is the difference between a design that works on paper and on the bench.

gm transconductance · mA/V

Calculated: central difference on the square-law model at the operating point, equal to gm0·(1−VGS/VP) with gm0=2·IDSS/|VP|. Enter a DCA75 gfs to cross-check.

Circuit impact: drain current per volt of gate swing — the engine of gain (Av = −gm·Rd bypassed). Falls as bias goes colder (toward VP).

Sonic levers: hotter bias (bigger IDSS device, smaller Rs) raises gm — more gain, earlier breakup. A low-VP part like the J201 gives high gm at tiny currents: the classic high-gain JFET front end.

rd drain resistance · kΩ

Calculated: from each device's typical channel-length-modulation factor λ as rd = 1/(λ·ID) (≈ VA/ID, the Early voltage VA = 1/λ) — so it scales with bias current. Override: type a measured rd in the device panel and the tool back-calculates λ = 1/(rd·ID) at the operating point, so rd, Zout, the gain ceiling, the drain-curve tilt and the time-domain sim all track your real part. Read it off a DCA75 curve trace as the saturation-region slope ΔVDS/ΔID (or extrapolate the curve back to ID=0 for VA); leave it blank to use the typical λ.

Circuit impact: the JFET's internal AC drain resistance, sitting in parallel with Rd at the output. With a small Rd it trims gain only slightly, but it caps the gain a large Rd can deliver (intrinsic limit gm·rd) and sets the output-Z ceiling — so it matters most in high-Rd, high-gain stages. A source follower is nearly rd-independent (Zout ≈ 1/gm), so the override barely moves its numbers.

Sonic levers: mostly a device trait — worth measuring when a high-gain stage's gain or output Z doesn't match the bench. Parts with high rd (low λ) let big drain resistors actually deliver their gain; with low rd, doubling Rd gives diminishing returns.

Gain, impedance & headroom
Gain (Av) mid-band · × / dB

Calculated: CS bypassed — −gm·(Rd ∥ rd ∥ Rload). CS unbypassed (Fetzer) — that, divided by (1+gm·Rs). Source follower — gm·Rs′/(1+gm·Rs′), always <1.

Circuit impact: the small-signal voltage gain actually applied to your input. Fetzer's rule of thumb: Av ≈ 0.5·Rd/Rs ≈ 0.54·(V+/|VP|−2) — note it rides on V+ and VP, not IDSS.

Sonic levers: the source bypass cap is the big one (see Harmonic law). Raising V+ buys gain without shrinking the clean window; a smaller Rs/bigger Rd ratio pushes gain at the cost of headroom.

Input Z mid-band · kΩ / MΩ

Calculated: Rg (gate stopper) + the gate return. J1: the gate resistor Rgate to ground; J2: the interstage pot's wiper-to-ground section, so it moves with Vol.

Circuit impact: the load this stage presents to its driver. The gate is essentially open at DC, so Rgate sets it — JFETs make naturally high-Z inputs (great right after a guitar pickup). HF input Z falls with Miller capacitance (Bode chart).

Sonic levers: bigger Rgate (1 M → 2.2 M) loads the pickup less — brighter, more open. Raise Rg to drop the Miller corner for a darker, smoother top.

Output Z mid-band · kΩ

Calculated: CS — Rd ∥ rd (unbypassed Rs raises it via degeneration). Source follower — Rs ∥ (1/gm), typically a few hundred Ω.

Circuit impact: the source impedance driving the next stage — sets the coupling-cap HP corner and how hard the next gate's conduction sags this stage.

Sonic levers: this is exactly why you'd set a stage to source follower — trade voltage gain for a low, stiff output that drives cables, tone stacks or the next pedal without treble loss. A CS stage's high Rd is the opposite: lots of gain, but soft drive.

Harmonic law power-law exponent

Calculated: exponent of the channel transfer referenced to cutoff (fit at two drive levels, ~0.4–0.8·|VP| above cutoff, re-solving the bias so degeneration counts). A bypassed source sits near 2.0; the Fetzer unbypassed Rs pulls it toward 1.5.

Circuit impact: the heart of the Fetzer Valve. A bare JFET is square-law (exponent 2.0, strong 2nd harmonic). Leaving Rs unbypassed applies local feedback that bends the curve toward the triode's three-halves law (1.5) — the same harmonic recipe as a 12AX7.

Sonic levers: add a source bypass cap (Cs) → exponent climbs toward 2.0, ~6 dB more gain, fatter 2nd-harmonic, less headroom. Pull it → toward 1.5, cleaner, more "tube-like," more touch dynamics. This single cap is the main voicing choice.

Max in / Output headroom · Vpp

Calculated: two asymmetric edges — gate driven up to conduction (VGS ≈ +0.6 V) and down to cutoff (VGS=VP) — mapped through the gain to an input window and an output swing, measured on the same load line the waveform uses.

Circuit impact: "Max in" is the biggest clean input before either edge clips (compare directly to the Input slider, also Vpp). "Output" is the swing actually delivered now, post-clipping.

Use it: compare J1's Output against J2's Max in — raise Vol until Output exceeds it and that's where the second stage starts to break up. The window is roughly ±|VP|, so a 2N5457 (big |VP|) takes hotter signals clean than a J201.

Dynamics
Gate shift & Block blocking distortion · V

Calculated: when the gate swings past ~+0.6 V the gate-channel junction conducts, charging the input coupling cap; the mean conduction current × the discharge resistance gives the DC bias displacement. The Block meter shows proximity to onset, then the volts of shift past it.

Circuit impact: the JFET's version of grid-current "blocking" — under heavy drive the bias drifts toward cutoff and the stage chokes/ducks until the cap drains. A DC-coupled stage (no cap) can't store the charge, so it can't block.

Sonic levers: a bigger gate stopper Rg limits the conduction current (gentler onset); a smaller coupling cap recovers faster (tighter, less "duck"). The classic high-gain trade between fullness and tightness.

Bias excursion source-cap drift · V

Calculated: mean instantaneous source voltage over the settled window minus its quiescent value — the bypass cap holds the source with τ=Rs·Cs, so asymmetric current drifts it away from idle.

Circuit impact: slow self-rebias under sustained signal — the compression component of "feel." Only present when a source bypass cap exists.

Sonic levers: bigger Cs lengthens the bloom/squash on held notes; remove it and the excursion vanishes (stiffer, faster, ~6 dB less gain — the Fetzer voicing).

Network · interstage & output
Attenuation / Source Z interstage divider

Calculated: standard pot-divider math at the Vol wiper — attenuation = Rpot_bot/(Rseries+Rpot); source Z = (Rseries+Rpot_top) ∥ Rpot_bot, peaking near mid-rotation.

Circuit impact: how much of J1's output reaches J2's gate, and the impedance feeding it (which, with J2's Miller capacitance, sets the HF rolloff and sits in the blocking-charge path).

Sonic levers: Vol decides where the chain distorts — low Vol keeps J2 clean, high Vol slams it (watch its Block meter). A bright cap across the pot's top half lifts the highs that the mid-rotation impedance peak would otherwise dull.

Master / Output Z output divider

Calculated: same divider math on the output network; Output Z = (Rseries+Rpot_top+Zout J2) ∥ Rpot_bot at the master wiper.

Circuit impact: sets output level after all distortion is generated, and the impedance the outside world sees. Set J2 to source follower and this Z drops dramatically — the buffered output.

Sonic levers: driving long cables or a fuzz from a high CS output shaves treble; a source-follower last stage (or running Master high / a smaller master pot) keeps the output stiff and bright.
